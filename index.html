Delegation Readiness Index (DRI) — v1.0

Written by Henning du Preez, 2025 · Licensed under CC BY 4.0

Purpose

The Delegation Readiness Index exists to operationalize The Delegation Law.

It provides a clear test for determining whether a decision may be delegated to machine intelligence without degrading the integrity of the system over time.

The index does not measure capability. It measures readiness.

Definition

Delegation Readiness Index (DRI) is a non-compensatory assessment composed of three independent dimensions:

DRI = Structural Alignment × Propagation Stability × System Integrity

Each dimension must hold independently. Failure in any single dimension results in delegation failure.

The index is multiplicative by design. Strength in one dimension cannot offset weakness in another.

Dimension I — Structural Alignment

Question it answers: Does delegating this decision remove a real system limitation, or merely automate around it?

Structural Alignment holds when:

  • The governing constraint is invalidated, not bypassed
  • Delegation increases system effectiveness, not just efficiency
  • Human bottlenecks are removed rather than relocated
  • The old rule no longer governs outcomes

Structural Alignment fails when:

  • Delegation accelerates an unchanged constraint
  • Human judgment remains the hidden bottleneck
  • Errors increase faster than insight
  • Speed substitutes for understanding

If Structural Alignment fails, delegation amplifies failure.

Dimension II — Propagation Stability

Question it answers: Does the decision remain sound as reliance spreads across users, systems, or time?

Propagation Stability holds when:

  • Outcomes remain stable under increased adoption
  • Misuse does not become the dominant mode
  • Behavioural reversion is limited and detectable
  • Coordination cost does not grow faster than value

Propagation Stability fails when:

  • Local success collapses at scale
  • Dependence introduces new systemic risks
  • Human vigilance erodes as delegation becomes normal
  • Edge cases become the norm

If Propagation Stability fails, delegation destabilizes the system.

Dimension III — System Integrity

Question it answers: When the machine is wrong, misused, or attacked, does the system recover?

System Integrity holds when:

  • Errors are bounded and visible
  • Decisions are auditable and attributable
  • Recovery paths exist and are exercised
  • Dependency does not hollow out human judgment

System Integrity fails when:

  • Errors compound silently
  • Accountability diffuses or disappears
  • Reversal is impractical or impossible
  • Long-term capability decays

If System Integrity fails, delegation causes irreversible damage.

Evaluation Rule

A decision may be delegated to machine intelligence if and only if:

  • Structural Alignment holds
  • Propagation Stability holds
  • System Integrity holds

If any dimension fails, delegation is structurally premature, regardless of performance or demand.

What DRI Is Not

  • DRI is not a benchmark.
  • It is not a maturity model.
  • It is not a safety checklist.
  • It is not a prediction of outcomes.

It does not rank systems. It governs decisions.

Use

DRI is applied at the point of delegation, before reliance becomes normal.

It is intended for: system designers, organizational leaders, regulators, boards, and anyone accountable for long-term outcomes.

DRI does not remove responsibility. It makes responsibility explicit.

Closing

Machine intelligence makes delegation inevitable. The Delegation Law makes it governable. DRI makes it decidable.

Delegation without readiness compounds risk. Delegation with readiness compounds value.
